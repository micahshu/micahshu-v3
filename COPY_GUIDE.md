# Copy Guide — micahshu.com

## Voice Principle

**Intimidating at scale. Friendly in the details.**

This mirrors the design system directly. Large display text — headlines, callouts, section titles — carries weight. It's declarative, confident, and terse. Small copy — descriptions, body paragraphs, hover text — is where personality lives. Warmer, more direct, occasionally dry, sometimes witty.

Neither register softens the other. The large text isn't hostile; the small text isn't weak.

---

## Core Positioning

Micah is a freelance full-stack developer building websites and web apps for local businesses in Northern Colorado. One developer, start to finish — no handoffs, no overhead, no mystery about who's doing the work.

**Through-line:** `One developer. Your whole project.`  
This is the spine of the site. Directness is the differentiator. You know exactly who you're hiring, and they own the entire thing.

**Dual audience:** This site serves two readers — potential freelance clients (local businesses, founders) and potential employers reviewing it as a portfolio. Client-facing copy leads on most pages. The work itself is the resume signal for employers — no need to write toward both audiences simultaneously.

**Current situation:** Micah is employed full-time at a marketing agency and freelances on the side. The agency angle is useful context for credibility on the About page, but should not be a recurring theme elsewhere.

## Tone Rules

**Do:**
- Write in short, declarative sentences. One idea per sentence.
- Use present tense. "I build" not "I have been building."
- Let confidence come from specificity, not volume. One sharp sentence beats three vague ones.
- Use the em dash for rhythm and compression. ("Better product, no overhead.")
- Write for someone skimming — the first clause of every sentence should carry the meaning.
- Be direct about who this is for: local businesses, founders, anyone who wants a real developer, not a template.

**Don't:**
- Use buzzwords: *passionate*, *innovative*, *solutions*, *synergy*, *leverage*, *seamless*.
- Explain what you do in passive voice. Not "websites are built" — "I build websites."
- Oversell. One honest sentence is better than three boastful ones.
- Write copy that could be on any freelancer's site. Test every line: could someone else say this?
- Use first-person in display/hero text. Large text is a statement about the world, not a diary entry.

## Voice at Each Scale

### Display / Hero (Bebas Neue, large)
Statements. Imperatives. Fragments. No hedging.  
Examples: `START A PROJECT.` / `FULL STACK DEVELOPER` / `MICAH SHU`

### Section Headings (h1/h2, Funnel Sans)
Still punchy. One strong clause. No filler.  
Examples: `What I build` / `How I work` / `Recent work`

### Subtext / Descriptions (body, muted)
This is where warmth lives. Complete sentences, but still tight. Can include a beat of humor or specificity.  
Examples: `One developer. Your whole project.` / `Building websites for small businesses in Northern Colorado.` / `Open to new projects & collaborations.`

### Metadata / Labels (caption/label size)
Pure function. Noun phrases only.  
Examples: `Based in Berthoud, CO` / `Available` / `5 Years Experience`

## Page-by-Page Intent

### Homepage
First impression. Establish the person and the pitch in seconds.
- Hero: name + title, one-line pitch. No paragraph.
- About strip: One strong sentence that earns curiosity. This is not the full bio — it's the hook.
- CTA: Assertive. "START A PROJECT." assumes they want one. Not "Interested in working together?"

### /about
Expand on the person, not just the resume. Micah has agency experience — that's the credibility.  The page should feel like a handshake, not a CV.
- The subtitle under the hero title is the most-read copy on the page. It should say something true and specific. Not "I love building things." True for everyone. 
- Micah is a father of four: oldest boy (3), twin boys (2), and a newborn girl (April 2026). Pages reference "father of four" — don't list ages inline, they go stale.

### /projects
Let the work talk. Copy is spare — title, one-line description, stack tags. No project should need more than two sentences to introduce.

### /services
Concrete. Scope, timeline, what's included. No vague tiers ("Starter / Professional / Enterprise"). If a service has a time range, say the range. Honesty builds trust faster than polish.

### /contact
Lowest barrier possible. Short, warm, direct. Not "Fill out this form and I'll get back to you within 3–5 business days." Just: here's how to reach me.

### /blog
Voice can relax here. More personal, more exploratory. Still tight sentences — this isn't a stream-of-consciousness journal. But longer paragraphs are fine. Opinions are good.

## Local SEO Strategy

> See `SEO.md` for full keyword data, competitor analysis, and recommended site structure. This section is the copy-writing summary only.

### Keyword Tiers

**Primary — homepage anchor:**  
`Fort Collins web developer` / `Fort Collins web design` / `Northern Colorado web developer` / `freelance web developer Fort Collins`  
Fort Collins is the only modifier with real volume. "Northern Colorado" is a strong secondary catch-all — underused by every competitor. "Freelance" modifier signals high buyer intent (actively not wanting an agency).

**Secondary — services pages:**  
`WordPress developer Fort Collins` / `small business website Fort Collins` / `affordable web design Fort Collins` / `custom website design Fort Collins`  
Each of these warrants its own page or dedicated section. Do not crowd them all onto the homepage.

**Quick wins — low competition, target early:**  
`hire web developer Fort Collins` / `freelance web developer Northern Colorado` / `affordable web design Northern Colorado`  
Near-zero dedicated competition. A well-optimized page can rank page 1 within 3–6 months.

**Berthoud — citations only, not a keyword strategy:**  
Search volume is functionally zero. Do not build pages targeting Berthoud terms. Use Berthoud for: Google Business Profile address, About/Contact copy ("based in Berthoud"), and local directory citations. The GBP alone will surface Micah for "web developer near me" queries from the I-25 corridor without any dedicated page.

### Positioning Note

Competitors Rachel (juicy-ideas.com) and Steve (stevethewebsiteguy.com) both use "affordable" framing. Micah should pair affordability with custom/full-stack to differentiate: `affordable custom web development`, not just `affordable web design`. The technical depth is the differentiator — lead with it.

### Where to Place Geographic Terms

- **Hero location tag** — `Berthoud, CO` as a personality/locality signal. Small label, not a keyword target.
- **Homepage H1/subtext** — `Fort Collins` or `Northern Colorado` in the primary pitch. This is the SEO anchor.
- **About page** — establish roots naturally: Berthoud, Front Range, the family. This is where local identity lives.
- **Services pages** — each page targets its own keyword; geographic modifier should appear in the H1 and first paragraph.
- **Contact page** — reinforce locality. "Based in Berthoud, serving Fort Collins and Northern Colorado."
- **Page `<title>` and meta** — every page needs a geographic modifier. `Micah Shu — Web Developer in Fort Collins, CO` not just `Micah Shu`.

### Rules

- Never stack multiple city names in one sentence. Reads like keyword stuffing.
- Geographic terms should sound like the copy wrote itself. "I build websites for small businesses in Fort Collins and Northern Colorado." Not "Fort Collins web developer serving Loveland Longmont Berthoud."
- Berthoud is personality copy. Fort Collins is positioning copy. They do different work — don't swap them.

## Recurring Copy Patterns

### Navigation descriptions (hero link blocks)
One clause. Describes what the page *does for the reader*, not what it contains.  
- About: `Who I am & how I work`  
- Projects: `Selected websites & apps`  
- Contact: `Open to new projects & collaborations`

### CTAs
Use the ↗ arrow (U+2197) on all outbound or navigation CTAs. Never →.  
Button labels are brief imperative phrases. No period.  
Examples: `Get in Touch` / `View Project ↗` / `Read More ↗`

### Status / Availability
Treated like a real signal, not marketing fluff. `Available` means available now. If that changes, update it.

## What Not to Write

| Avoid | Instead |
|---|---|
| "I'm passionate about creating digital experiences" | "I build websites that work." |
| "Let's connect and explore synergies" | "Open to new projects." |
| "Helping businesses achieve their goals online" | "Websites for small businesses in Northern Colorado." |
| "I wear many hats" | List the hats |
| "Full-service digital solutions" | Say what you actually do |
| "Quality work delivered on time" | Every freelancer says this. Say something true |
